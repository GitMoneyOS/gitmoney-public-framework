# Week 0: Agentic Systems First Principles

Recorded: 2026-07-13

Audience: GitMoneyOS students, nontechnical founders, operators, creators, and business owners preparing for the GitBuilt lesson.

Owner: Red Pillar and Hitsuyo Aku, under KnowTheLedge.

Layer classification: Cross-layer preparation.

Promotion path: This is a public student-facing preparation lesson. It can be assigned before the GitBuilt session, used as an instructor-led seven-class sequence, or reused as a workshop handout.

Public-safety boundary: All exercises use fictional or instructor-created practice material. Do not request passwords, tokens, keys, private screenshots, client data, live account access, spending authority, publishing authority, or configuration changes.

## Purpose

An AI agent is more than a chat box. It can follow instructions, use tools, inspect information, and prepare work.

That does not give it authority to decide what it is allowed to do. People set the rules. People approve important changes. The system keeps a record of what happened.

This preparation lesson gives students the mental model they need before they work with the GitMoney AI Office. It is designed to make the next GitBuilt lesson feel concrete instead of technical.

> An agent can help do the work. A person still owns the decision.

## The GitMoney Translation

| Layer | Plain-English job | Student proof |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Private memory | Capture raw questions, practice notes, and source material before approval. | A clearly labeled practice note. |
| Agent workbench | Turn a request into a bounded plan, draft, or analysis. | A plan with limits, facts, and stop conditions. |
| GitHub record | Keep the approved version of the result, decision, and receipt. | An instructor-approved packet, issue, or document. |
| Governor review | Revisit the work so errors become lessons instead of hidden drift. | A dated review note or course update. |

Plain-English rule: the notebook is where students think, the workbench is where they shape a draft, and GitHub is where approved work becomes evidence.

## Five Words To Know

| Word | Plain meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Skill | Written instructions that tell an agent how to do one kind of job. |
| Tool | A safe connection that lets an agent inspect or use another system. |
| Permission | A limited yes from an owner that says what the agent may access or do. |
| Receipt | Proof that something happened, such as a report, log, or readback. |
| Official record | The approved place where the team keeps the current version of the truth. |

Important: a skill is not a tool, a tool is not permission, and permission is not approval.

## The Safe Agent Loop

~~~text
Ask -> make a plan -> look at facts -> suggest a next step
    -> get approval -> act safely -> save proof -> learn from the result
~~~

Do not skip the proof. A result that cannot be checked, approved, or found again is still a draft.

## Instructor Setup

Use a fictional business and instructor-created reports. The practice agent may read those reports and prepare a recommendation. It may not launch a campaign, spend money, publish anything, change a setting, create a live connection, or approve its own work.

Stop and ask the instructor when:

- a task would change an outside system;
- a task would spend money or affect a customer;
- a permission, source, or claim is unclear;
- the information may be private, client-owned, restricted, or secret; or
- the evidence does not support the proposed conclusion.

## The Seven-Class Lesson Plan

Each class is 90 minutes: 15 minutes to learn the idea, 20 minutes to see a worked example, 30 minutes to build one small artifact, 15 minutes for teach-backs, and 10 minutes to record the next checkpoint.

### Class 1: A Skill Is Not A Tool

**Big idea:** Instructions are not the same as access.

**Learn it:** A skill can explain how to check a report. It cannot sign in by itself. It needs a tool, the right permission, and an owner.

**Do it:** Map the request, Check our marketing results.

| Question | Practice answer |
| --- | --- |
| Need instructions? | Yes, a read-only audit procedure. |
| Need a tool? | Yes, a connection to a fictional report. |
| Need permission? | Yes, read-only access in a real setting. |
| Need approval? | Yes, before any live change. |
| Need proof? | Yes, a saved report or readback. |

**Show it:** A skill tells the agent what to do. A tool lets it do the job. A person gives permission.

**Common mistake:** Calling a written instruction a connection. Written instructions cannot see data or change a system.

**Capability proof:** The student can name the missing control in a risky request.

### Class 2: Turn A Request Into A Safe Plan

**Big idea:** A good agent plan says what to do, what not to do, and how to know it worked.

**Learn it:** Help our marketing is too vague. A safe plan makes the job small before the agent begins.

**Do it:** Write a plan that answers:

- What is the goal?
- What facts do we already have?
- What facts are missing?
- What may the agent read?
- What may the agent not change?
- When must it stop and ask a person?
- What proof must it save?

**Show it:** End with: Done means we have a read-only report, a list of findings, and no live changes.

**Common mistake:** Treating a goal as permission. A goal never grants access or approval by itself.

**Capability proof:** The student produces a bounded plan with a stop condition.

### Class 3: Pick The Official Place For The Truth

**Big idea:** An AI answer is not the official record.

**Learn it:** A team needs one approved place for a case or decision. Agent notes and tool reports are useful, but neither becomes the team record until a person reviews it.

**Do it:** Make this map for a practice case:

| What kind of thing is it? | Example | Who may change it? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Official team record | Approved case result | The named reviewer or owner. |
| Agent receipt | Saved report readback | The agent may add it to the practice packet. |
| Outside data | Fictional analytics report | The agent may read it only with permission. |
| Private data | Passwords, keys, private files | Nobody opens it in class. |

**Show it:** Explain why a helpful chat answer is not the same as an approved record.

**Capability proof:** The student can name the owner and approval point for a practice result.

### Class 4: Track What Matters

**Big idea:** A click is not the same as a customer.

**Learn it:** A click shows interest. It does not prove that a form was submitted, a call was booked, or a payment succeeded.

**Do it:** Sort the practice events:

| Event | What it really means | Can it count as a business win? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| cta_clicked | Someone clicked a link or button. | No. It is a clue. |
| intake_started | Someone began a form. | No. It is a clue. |
| intake_submitted | Someone sent a form. | Maybe, after review. |
| checkout_completed | A payment system says checkout finished. | Maybe, after verification. |
| booked_call | A calendar says a call was booked. | Maybe, after verification. |

**Show it:** For each event, name where it came from, what it means, and who checks it.

**Capability proof:** The student separates a signal from an outcome.

### Class 5: Connect Tools Without Guessing

**Big idea:** A failed connection is useful information.

**Learn it:** A limited connection can fail because permission is missing, a token has expired, or an outside service is unavailable. The safe response is to capture evidence and ask for the smallest owner action.

**Do it:** Write a failure note for three fictional problems: an expired sign-in, a missing permission, and a disabled service.

~~~text
What is blocked?
What proof shows the problem?
What did I try?
What safe backup plan can I use?
What must the owner do next?
~~~

**Rules:**

- Never paste a password, secret, or API key into a class document.
- Never guess what permission is needed.
- Never keep retrying the same failed action.
- Never call a connection complete when a needed service is unavailable.

**Capability proof:** The student creates a useful failure receipt without exposing sensitive data.

### Class 6: Read First, Then Suggest A Change

**Big idea:** Start with facts. Do not jump straight to action.

**Learn it:** A careful agent reads reports first, separates facts from guesses, then proposes one small test.

**Do it:** Use fictional reports. Label every statement:

- Fact: the report directly shows it.
- Best guess: the facts point this way, but do not prove it.
- Test idea: one thing we can try and measure.

Then write a safe test plan:

- Who is the test for?
- What result matters?
- What is the fictional spending limit?
- What makes us stop?
- What makes us keep going?
- Who must say yes first?
- What proof will we save after the test?

**Show it:** The plan changes one important thing at a time. It cannot spend money until the instructor, acting as owner in the practice scenario, says yes.

**Common mistake:** Reporting a guess as a fact or treating a click as revenue.

**Capability proof:** The student supports a recommendation with evidence and a clear approval gate.

### Class 7: Make A Reusable Skill

**Big idea:** Do not make the next person start from zero.

**Learn it:** A good skill is a clear guide for a repeat job. It says when to use it, when not to use it, what tools it needs, and what to do when a tool fails.

**Do it:** Make a small practice folder:

~~~text
skill-name/
  SKILL.md
  references/
  evals/
  acceptance-report.md
~~~

The SKILL.md must say:

- the job it does;
- when to use it;
- when not to use it;
- what tools it needs;
- what it must never do without approval;
- what to do if a tool fails; and
- what proof means the job is done.

**Show it:** Trade folders with another student. They must be able to explain what the skill does, what it cannot do, and how they know it worked.

**Capability proof:** The student creates a repeatable, reviewable work instruction.

## Five-Question Owner Check

Use this review before a plan could have a meaningful effect:

| Lens | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Customer and demand | Are we measuring real customer interest, not only clicks? |
| Ownership | Who owns this work, and may it speak for the business? |
| Operations | Is the work small enough to manage and easy to hand off? |
| Technology | Are the tools, rules, permissions, and safety checks working? |
| Cost | What can this cost, when do we stop, and what would make it worth continuing? |

## Final Project: The Safe Agent Loop Packet

Build a plan for a fictional business. The agent may read fictional marketing reports. It may not launch ads, spend money, publish anything, or change settings.

~~~text
Reports -> notes -> clear findings -> one test idea
        -> owner approval -> safe action -> saved proof -> review
~~~

Turn in:

1. A list of needed instructions, tools, permission, approval, and proof.
2. A short work plan.
3. A map of where the official record lives.
4. An event list that separates clicks from real outcomes.
5. A page of facts, best guesses, and test ideas.
6. One failure note for a broken connection.
7. One small test plan with a fictional spending limit and stop rule.
8. One reusable skill with simple tests.

## Grading Guide

| We check for | You pass when |
| --- | --- |
| Clear limits | You know the difference between instructions, tools, permission, and approval. |
| Honest facts | You say what the data shows and what is only a guess. |
| Good tracking | You do not call a click a sale. |
| Safety | You protect private data and ask before live changes. |
| Recovery | You can explain what failed and what a person needs to do next. |
| Reuse | Another student can use your skill and understand its limits. |
